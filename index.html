Waves

Waves carry energy and information through a medium — or, for light, through vacuum. Amplitude measures how strongly the medium is displaced; frequency counts how many oscillations occur each second.

In water and air, waves reflect at boundaries (e.g. a shoreline) and can reinforce or cancel where they meet — that is interference.

Classic demos: longitudinal and transverse waves on a spring (“slinky”), sound in air, and surface waves on water.

Focus: v = fλ · interference · reflection

Electric field

Every electric charge creates an electric field around itself — a way to describe the force another charge would feel, depending on distance and geometry.

Fields are often drawn as lines inspired by the direction a small positive test charge would move. Closer line spacing means a stronger field in that region.

Coulomb’s law gives the force between point charges; Gauss’s law helps compute flux through closed surfaces when symmetry is simple.

Focus: E · potential · capacitance · energy in the field

Magnetic field

Permanent magnets and currents in wires produce a magnetic field B. Field lines help predict the force on the north pole of an ideal small compass.

In the 1800s, Faraday and others found that a changing magnetic flux through a coil induces a voltage — the principle behind generators and many modern sensors.

Together with the electric field, electromagnetic induction and electromagnetic waves build a single picture of light and radio.

Focus: B · Lorentz force · electromagnetic induction

Radioactivity

Inside some nuclei, nuclear forces cannot bind every combination of protons and neutrons stably; the nucleus may decay by emitting particles or radiation.

α particles (helium-4 nuclei), β⁺ or β⁻ electrons, and high-energy γ photons penetrate matter very differently — shielding is designed accordingly.

Activity A (in becquerel) counts decays per unit time; half-life T½ tells you how quickly an isotope’s activity drops.

Focus: decay · half-life · dosimetry · shielding

Space physics

A satellite around Earth or a comet approaching the Sun follows paths described as orbits, often approximated as conic sections using Kepler’s laws.

Newton’s law of universal gravitation gives the force between two masses; the speed needed for a low Earth orbit is often quoted around v ≈ 7.9 km/s (depending on altitude).

Modern cosmology links galaxy observations to models of cosmic expansion and to dark matter and dark energy.

Focus: orbits · energy · gravitation

Waves — beyond everyday intuition

Waves connect music, the sea, optics, and communications in one mathematical language.

In physics, a wave is a disturbance propagating from place to place. Wavelength λ, frequency f, and wave speed v satisfy v = fλ, one of the most useful relationships in wave analysis.

Waves can be transverse (displacement perpendicular to propagation, like light in vacuum) or longitudinal (displacement parallel, like sound in air in a simple one-dimensional picture).

Energy and amplitude
For many linear waves, power scales with amplitude squared — amplifying a signal increases the energy it carries.
Reflection and refraction
At a boundary between media with different refractive indices, some energy reflects and some refracts according to Snell’s law in optics.
Modern examples
Sonar, radar, medical ultrasound, and radio transmitters use different frequencies to measure distance or carry information.

Project ideas

  • Demo with a water tray or a spring to show reflection and standing waves.
  • Measure sound frequency with a phone app; compare to a resonant tube length.
  • Sketch constructive/destructive interference from two sources on water (if a lab is available).

For a presentation, add graphs (amplitude vs. time) or photos from a simple class demo.

Electric field — from vector E to a simple circuit

Charges create fields; fields describe force on every new charge.

Electric field strength at a point measures how strongly the field would push a unit positive charge. In dielectric materials, the field inside is usually reduced compared with vacuum, depending on the dielectric constant.

Electric potential V and potential difference ΔV relate to work moving charge between two points. That is why stored energy in a capacitor depends on the field between plates and on capacitance C.

Coulomb & Gauss (ideas)
Coulomb’s law gives force between point charges. Gauss’s law simplifies flux calculations when there is spherical, cylindrical, or planar symmetry.
Energy in the field
In many setups, stored energy is tied to integrating field strength over the region where the field is non-negligible.
Everyday safety
High voltage without good insulation risks shock or arc flash; grounding and protective devices are standard in modern wiring.

Project ideas

  • Sketch field lines for two opposite charges and a neutral region between them.
  • Explain why two charged parallel plates produce an approximately uniform field between them.
  • Research a simple everyday example (static hair, a lighter spark).

A short animation of current, voltage, and resistance can connect to Ohm’s law as an extension.

Magnetic field — from magnets to induction

B, currents, and changing flux — motors, generators, and hospital MRI.

Magnetic field B describes how a magnet or a current affects moving charges. Right-hand rules help predict field direction for simple straight wires.

Changing magnetic flux Φ through a closed loop (e.g. a coil) induces an emf — the discovery behind transformers, dynamos, and many contactless sensors.

For charge q moving at velocity v in field B, the Lorentz force is F = q (v × B), a bridge between electromagnetism and mechanics.

Magnetic materials
Ferromagnets (like iron) greatly enhance B compared with air; paramagnets weakly enhance it; diamagnets weakly oppose an applied field.
Tokamaks and MRI (advanced examples)
Strong fields confine plasma or align proton spins in medical imaging — same core physics, very different engineering.

Project ideas

  • With a magnet, coil, and LED, show a voltage pulse when the magnet moves.
  • Sketch force direction for a proton entering perpendicular to uniform B.
  • Discuss why transformers use an iron core and insulation between windings.

Keep classroom demos at low voltage and with supervision.

Radioactivity — decay, measurement, and responsibility

Unstable nuclei decay; we measure, describe, and shield.

α particles are heavy and stopped easily (e.g. by paper); β penetrates a few centimetres in tissue; γ photons need denser shielding (lead, concrete) for strong attenuation.

Half-life T½ is the time for activity to drop by half; after n half-lives, activity is (½)n of the original. This underpins carbon-14 dating and nuclear waste timelines.

Activity is measured in becquerel (Bq), decays per second; biological dose uses units like gray and sievert that depend on energy absorbed and radiation type.

Common applications
Medicine (PET, targeted therapy), industrial gauges, and nuclear power are professional domains — always with strict regulation.
Protection (summary)
Time, distance, and shielding: limit exposure, stay farther from sources when possible, and use appropriate materials.

Project ideas

  • Comparison table: α, β, γ — penetration, charge, example isotope.
  • Ethical presentation: pros and cons of nuclear energy with cited sources.
  • Without a counter, explain in words how a Geiger–Müller tube works.

Do not handle unknown radioactive sources; at school use only models, figures, and verified data.

Space physics — orbits, mass, and the cosmos

From throwing a stone to galaxies: the same gravitation, different scales.

For many problems Earth is treated as a large central mass and the satellite as small — orbital energy and ellipse size follow Kepler-style relations (e.g. T² ∝ a³ for the solar system, used with care).

Escape speed from a body follows from kinetic and gravitational potential energy; reaching low Earth orbit needs large speed, which is why multi-stage rockets burn for many minutes.

The cosmic microwave background and galactic redshift are two strong lines of evidence for an expanding universe studied with ground- and space-based telescopes.

Newton & Kepler
Newton showed an inverse-square force gives elliptical planetary orbits; Kepler had described the motion empirically from data first.
Modern missions ISS, JWST, planetary probes
They extend what we can observe — but the same mathematical foundations you learn in school still apply.

Project ideas

  • Compute vcirc ≈ √(GM/r) for a low Earth orbit using tabulated G, M, r.
  • Compare orbital periods for two satellites at different radii.
  • Short report on Voyager, Cassini, or a Mars rover with mission milestones.

Precise numbers depend on the model (centre-to-centre distance, mass distribution); state your approximations clearly.
